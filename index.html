National Overview  ·  Sector Split

Two in Three Schools
Are Government-Run

Government schools account for the clear majority of Australia's schools nationally. Catholic schools form the largest non-government sector, while independent schools, ranging from elite private colleges to small community schools, make up the remainder.

By State  ·  School Density

The ACT Punches Far Above Its Weight

Adjusted for land area, the Australian Capital Territory is by far the most school-dense state in the country, a function of its tiny footprint and fully urban population. Circle size is proportional to the number of schools per 1,000 km². The bigger the circle, the denser the schools, meaning more schools squeezed into every 1,000 km² of land.

By State  ·  School Type

Primary Schools Lead in Every State and Territory

Primary schools outnumber secondary and combined schools in every state, reflecting Australia's demographic spread. Combined schools, catering to both primary and secondary students are especially common in smaller states and rural areas where student numbers are too low to sustain separate campuses.

Each group of bars represents a state, with teal for primary schools, pink for secondary, and dark blue for combined. Click the buttons at the top to show or hide each school type, so you can focus on just the level you care about. Hover over any bar to see the exact count.

Private Schooling  ·  State Comparison

Which States Have the Most Private Schools?

Raw counts favour the biggest states, but when comparing the share of non-government schools, a different picture emerges. Some smaller states have a surprisingly high proportion of Catholic and independent schools relative to their total.

2001–2025  ·  State × Sector

Every State Tells a Different Growth Story

Beneath the national trend lies a patchwork of regional stories. Independent schools have grown in every state, but the pace varies dramatically. The ACT and NT, with their unique demographics, show patterns that look nothing like the eastern states. Each panel shows the 25-year school count trajectory for Government, Catholic, and Independent sectors, with y-axes scaled independently so small states are as readable as large ones.

2001–2025  ·  Sector Growth

Independent Schools Have Grown by 23% Since 2001

While government school numbers fell through the 2000s before slowly recovering, independent schools have grown steadily for two decades, accelerating noticeably after 2015. Catholic schools have remained broadly stable. The overall picture is a slow but consistent shift away from government schooling.

Source: ACARA School Numbers Dataset 2001–2025. National totals only. Three lines, one per sector. Hover any point to see the exact school count for that year.

Gender  ·  Enrolment Split

Boys Outnumber Girls in Every State, But Only Just

Across all eight states and territories, male students slightly outnumber female students in 2025. The gap is consistent but narrow, no state deviates more than 1.5 percentage points from a perfectly equal split. The ACT has the widest gap; Tasmania the narrowest.

Data: ACARA Student Numbers Dataset 2025. All sectors, all school levels. Dashed line marks the 50/50 midpoint. States sorted by male share, descending.

Remoteness  ·  Access & Equity

Most Schools Are in Cities - But Who Serves the Outback?

The vast majority of Australia's schools are concentrated in major cities, yet government schools bear almost all the responsibility for educating students in remote and very remote communities.

Most schools are concentrated in major cities and inner regional areas. Very remote communities have a fraction of the options.

Government schools are the only sector with significant presence in remote and very remote areas. Catholic and independent schools are almost exclusively urban.
